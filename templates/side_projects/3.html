My exposure to programming was quite limited until about 6 months ago. I was not a complete noob, because I had taken some programming classes (C programming during undergrad and machine learning during grad) but I didn't know all the details and uses of coding, certainly how ridiculously hard it can be. About six months ago (now it is June 2022) I realized that I want to switch my field and learn how to code professionally and land an appropriate position. So I made a game plan: take programming courses on Udemy and follow through.

First online course: PHP

For some weird reason, I started (November 2021) with a PHP course. I wouldn't say that it was a complete waste of my time because I got to see what PHP is, how it is used and how powerful it is in maintaining the functionality of the websites. The content of the class was great; by the end of it, I built a fully-operational CMS (Content Management System) and got quite familiar with PHP. After completing and reflecting on the course, I thought that it was not that big of a challenge because the stuff I did was quite doable with enough patience and some Googling. I was acquainted with these sorts of problems during my studies (challenging but I knew that they are not unsolvable). Regardless, though, the learning process was satisfying; I was able to practice perseverance and discipline, which allowed me to finish the whole course in about two months.

Towards a harder challenge

Following the PHP course, I wanted to taste what software development is like, namely, what the process of hiring looks like. Prior to this, I had heard about interviews with some coding challenges, but nothing more than that. So I searched and came across the "Master the Coding Interview: Data Structures + Algorithms" course. It seemed interesting and something that can be challenging, so I signed up. Since it was about one week after I had completed the previous course on PHP, I was quite self-confident that new class will not be difficult. Oh, boy... how wrong was I...

There were so many new concepts that I hadn't known about programming: trees, stacks, dynamic programming, search algorithms, and others. Thankfully, I had not only arrogance leftover from the first course, but also the habit of patience and perseverance, which again helped me grind through the class in about a month. By the end of it, I was proud of myself. I was able to get accustomed to the 'coding world', or so I thought...

An impossible challenge

"Now that I have finished two classes, I can conquer the whole programming world" was the thought in my head. So I looked for some coding challenges, and why not go to Google for it. As it appeared to be, Google is quite active in holding various coding competitions on a very regular and frequent basis. I ended up signing for their kickstarter challenge. Although I knew that I had no chance of finishing among the top participants, I hoped I could solve 3 out of 4 problems.

However, when the day came, for which I was all excited and confident (solved like only two problems from the suggested problem sets, talk to me about that ignorance... ), I couldn't solve a single problem during the competition, and even after when I worked on it later. That was a disaster for me. I thought that I had had it, but apparently, I did not.

That experience has shown me that I still had a long way to go. After wallowing in a failure for some time, I slowed down my horses and modified my game plan. First, I wanted to get deeper a knowledge of Python and its capabilities. And second of all, I still wanted to learn how to be good at solving coding challenges.

Recovery from a hard fall

First of a all, for recovery, I needed to attempt something that wasn't as hard as that Google challenge. After searching, I found a course teaching Python, "100 Days of Code: The complete Python Pro Bootcamp for 2022". The course material covered a wide range of topics from very basic stuff to more specialized uses of Python (for data science, web scraping, and web development). It was good. As I started this course, my self-worth got better because I could actually solve small challenges that were given throughout the course. I learned quite a lot of new ways Python can be used.

Upon completion of this course (which took me about 2 months), I acquired experience in using Python for building apps with GUI, using BeautifulSoup for web scraping, using Flask for building blog websites, and doing data science. As an outcome of this class, I was able to create this page (to be more precise, enhance it to my own desires), start on the "Digitizing graphs" project, and use Python at work for the analysis of experimental data. Overall, my passion got a second breath after completing this course.

As I was using Python actively for my side projects and work, I also became a regular at Leetcode. After solving some problems, I realized that coding problems from the Google challenge were on some other level and that Leetcode is the place for me to grow in solving data structures and algorithm problems. I still suck, but a little less than before. I am happy to say that I am on an improving curve.

Now and next

All of the experiences have taught me not to get arrogant about my skills and at the same time not to get too discouraged when I can't solve something. They taught me to keep pushing, be realistic about my abilities, never stop learning, and that with enough time and effort put in there is nothing too hard to solve. I am planning to stick to this motto throughout my coding journey and life in general.

If you are still here with me, I'd like to thank you for making it to the end. If you have any questions, typos you saw, or suggestions, do not hesitate to let me know!
